Why is the theory of evolution questionable?

The ancient molecule, GK-PID, caused unicellular organisms to evolve into multicellular organisms about 800 million years ago. It was found that the GK-PID molecule acted as a "molecular carbine": it collected the chromosomes together and fixed them on the inner wall of the cell membrane when the division occurred. A fascinating discovery indicates that the ancient version of GK-PID behaved earlier than it does now. The reason why it turned into a "genetic carbine" is associated with a small genetic mutation that reproduced itself. It turns out that the origins of multicellular life forms is the result of one particular mutation.

The Earth Has a Second Moon? NASA Says Yes

In 2016 scientists discovered a “quasi-satellite” of our planet, which named HO3. It orbits the sun in such a way that the space rock never strays too far from Earth. The asteroid’s exact size is unknown, but researchers believe that it is between 130 feet and 330 feet wide. The asteroid poses no threat to earth, and never gets closer than 9 million miles to our planet.

Cryptography: Challenge for lovers

In January 2016, mathematicians opened a new prime number in the framework of the "Great Internet Mersenne Prime Search". This is 274207281 - 1. Modern cryptography uses Mersenne's prime numbers to decipher encoded information. 274207281 at the moment is the longest of all existing prime numbers (it is longer than its predecessor by almost 5 million digits). The total number of digits that make up a new prime number is about 24,000,000.

Information permafrost

Bernard Fisher

In God we trust. All others must have data.
At the moment there is no way that would allow storing data on one device for a really long period of time. Or does it exist? Recently, scientists from Southampton University made an amazing discovery. They used nano-structured glass in order to successfully create a process of recording and retrieving data. The storage device is a small glass disk the size of a 25-cent coin, which is capable of storing 360 terabytes of data and is not affected by high temperatures (up to 1000 degrees Celsius). The average shelf-life at room temperature is about 13.8 billion years (about the same time there is our universe). The data is recorded on the device using an ultrafast laser by means of short, intense light pulses.
